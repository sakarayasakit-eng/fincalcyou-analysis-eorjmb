Conventional vs Islamic, and expat rules

  • The QAR is pegged to the US dollar, so Qatari rates track the US Fed and the QCB rate (4.35%). Expect 5–6% for both conventional and Islamic finance.
  • Islamic finance (QIB, Masraf Al Rayan) uses Murabaha/Ijara — no interest, a transparent profit margin instead, at a similar effective cost.
  • Expats can buy in designated freehold areas; banks usually require a qualifying salary and a down payment of around 20–25%.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is home finance installment calculated in Qatar?

Using the reducing-balance formula P × r × (1+r)ⁿ ÷ ((1+r)ⁿ − 1). Islamic finance reaches a similar monthly figure via a profit margin (Murabaha) or lease (Ijara) instead of interest.

What is the current home finance rate in Qatar?

Around 5–6% per year in 2026. Because the QAR is pegged to the US dollar, rates track the US Fed and the QCB lending rate of 4.35%.

What is the difference between conventional and Islamic home finance?

Conventional charges interest on the balance. Islamic finance (QIB, Masraf Al Rayan) structures the deal as a sale at a fixed margin or a lease-to-own, avoiding riba. The monthly cost is broadly similar.

Can expats get home finance in Qatar?

Yes, in designated freehold zones, subject to a qualifying salary, residency, and typically a 20–25% down payment. Maximum age at maturity and salary thresholds vary by bank.

How much down payment do I need?

Commonly 20–25% for expats. A larger down payment lowers your monthly installment and total cost, and can improve the rate you're offered.
